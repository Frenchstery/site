Short fiction based on this prompt:

A legendary sword of power was lost in the final battle, shattered into many pieces... Thousands of years later, its broken shards ended up in the recycling plant, and repurposed into mundane, everyday items for the modern family.

“Mummy, what does return me to my bread men mean?” I asked Mum as she sat hypnotised by the TV.

“I don’t know Izzie,” she said without even looking at me. I sighed and asked,

“What’s for dinner tonight?”

With a wave of her hand, she said “Go bother Daddy, I’ve been at work all day.”

“Can I have toast?” Mrs Bell once told me five-year-olds shouldn’t be making toast, but I wanted to hear him again.

“Yes yes whatever, make it yourself then.” I smiled and charged into the kitchen. Daddy was sat at the table drinking another beer with his work friend. They didn’t even say hello as I circled round them toward the cupboard, I was too quick for them to even see me. My chair screeched as I dragged it to the worktop. I pulled him out, plugged him in and grinned at myself in his shiny side. Turning my ear toward him, I listened for his voice… nothing.

“Come on Mr Toaster, don’t be shy.”

“Playin’ that game again, eh?” Daddy’s friend said, he had a funny voice like the men who wear skirts to Remembrance Day.

“It’s not a game!”

“Izzie, where are your manners?”

“Sorry.” Bottom lip stuck out, I moped over to the bread bin and grabbed two slices. I whispered to him, “I know what will cheer you up,” and dropped the bread in his two mouths.

Return me to my brethren, he said. My face lit up. But I had to be sneaky. I didn’t want him to get taken away again.

“Daddy do you know what bread men are?”

“No Izzie. Nigel and I are trying to have a conversation here and you keep interrupting.”

With my back to Dad, I whispered back to the toaster, “How do I help you find your bread men?”

The knife, Izzie. Feed it to me. I looked up at the butter knife on the worktop, then at his mouths. The bread was gone.

“Daddy what happens if you put a-”

“Izzie! What did I just say?” Nigel’s neck-lump moved up and down as he swallowed. I trusted Mr Toaster, he always listened to me. Slowly, I reached for the knife. With its metal handle in my hand, I stabbed the sharp end into a mouth.

“ISABELLE JANE FABULAY” was the last thing I heard. I couldn’t move. It was like tiny bees were inside me trying to break through, all the way up my arm and back down to my foot. I opened my eyes. It stopped.

DO YOU KNOW WHERE YOU ARE IZZIE? said the toaster, not whispering like a squeaky mouse anymore but thundering like a mammoth. Not that I’d seen a mammoth, I’m not old enough. I was in the kitchen, it was dusty, the ceiling was collapsed and the walls were in pieces. Planes flew overhead. Sirens weered and wooed. I felt the air hit me like a huge wave followed by a bang in the distance. The sky was on fire.

“Where am I!” I shouted, tearing up.

THIS IS YOUR FUTURE.

THIS IS WHY I MUST BE RETURNED.

YOU WILL NOT STOP IT WITHOUT ME

I squeezed my eyes shut to shed a tear. I opened them again and Dad’s face was closer to me than it had ever been. We were almost touching. He pulled back.

“Izzie oh thank God.” I was huddled up on the ground with Dad kneeling over me. A hand grabbed his shoulder.

“Now Mr Fabulay sir, we’re gonna need to have a word with you.” A voice said. Standing behind Dad was a policeman. Dad turned red, red like he’d been lying face down on hot coals.

“WORDS?” he shouted “There’s nothing to talk about copper! Can’t you see it was an accident?”

I didn’t want to see this again. While they were shouting at each other, I stood up. Looked behind me. He was still there. The toaster. I had to get him before they realised, before they took him away from me again. I yanked him out of the socket and sprinted out the kitchen door. I peered into the living room, Mum, still hypnotised by the TV, shouted “Keep it down!”

Trust me, the toaster said. I looked into his shiny side. We must leave. I looked back, Mum hadn't moved, Dad was still shouting at the policeman. I looked at the front door. And ran straight for it

Short fiction based on this prompt:

As you stand on the edge of your balcony, ready to end your life, the ghost that's been living in your house decides to make an appearance. They've become quite fond of you.

"Paradise, I'm coming" I mumbled.

"What are you doing?" an echoing voice exclaimed. I lifted my head up from the flashing lights and faint sirens below.

"Huh?" I mumbled as I meerkatted my head around the balcony. I saw nothing behind me but empties; whisky, rye and resignation dotted lines. It's nothing, I thought.

"I ain't nothing pal," the voice said coming from a chair beside the table with the empties on.

I looked at the chair and said "Hallucinations. I've truly lost it now," and took a step closer to the edge, the blurred blue lights, the whimpering sirens.

"Oi!" it shouted. "Hold on mate, where do you think you're going? The concrete down there's four hundred foot away"

"That's the point," I said. Looking closer at the chair I noticed a wispy silhouette as solid as the condensation leaving my lips.

"You ain't thinking about offing yourself are yer? Cause that would make my afterlife so bloody boring. You're the most interesting cunt whose ever lived in this flat."

I shook my head. "You're just saying that."

"Just saying that?" he said and the smokey silhouette blew back a bit. "I'm not just saying anything. I've never laughed so hard as I have when watching you live here." Biting me with fangs of ice, the cold wind blew my hair across my face yet did nothing to the wispy silhouette. With a smile I took a step toward it and said,

"You mean you think I’m funny?”

“Yes pal, you leave me in stitches. Especially the way that every time you argue with your missus and she leaves, you huddle up under your duvet and cry like you’re a kid hiding from the dark.” The smile leapt off my face. Within the head of the silhouette, wine red eyes formed like two final embers of a fire.

“What.”

It continued, “Yeah and when you come in from work all stressed so you have to go to the mirror and give yourself a pep talk all like You are not a sex offender, the supervisor is just after sympathy. And you say it three times as if you’re trying to summon the candyman. It’s like I’m watching a live sitcom sometimes.”

I scrunched up my face, “Fuck you you’re not real, you’re a sick part of me” I said taking a step closer to the edge.

“Am I now?” it said and the condensation quickly condensed into something dense, a person. Almost. But it was familiar. He was familiar. I thought back to when I first bought the flat, when I moved in with my then girlfriend. Before I wasn’t chained to a desk at work out of public view, before I couldn’t see my daughter, before liver cirrhosis. He was a spitting image of a crusty painting we found in the attic when we first moved in. I felt the colour on my face leave with the wind. He said, “See because the thing is, I don’t think you’d have the bollocks to tell yourself that people who aren’t sex offenders don’t have to resign.” I closed my eyes, took a deep breath and a step forward.

“Is that why I ‘ant see yer daughter in a while?” Hoping it would be the one to send me over the edge, I took another step. My stomach leapt up to my throat as I felt nothing left to support me. The wind swept over me faster and faster like a rising blizzard. Siren shrills grew louder until I could hear nothing else. It was then that I opened my eyes. I was on the edge my balcony still. I swivelled around to look behind me. He was still sat there, but now he was in full colour; he was real. I felt numb at the sight of him. I couldn’t feel the floor. I couldn’t feel anything. I looked down. I wasn’t even touching the floor.

“Finally.” He said with a welcoming smile. “Glad you could join me.”

Poem on how technology affects our lives today

Sync

We are all in sync

Post-to-post, link-to-link

But what if they weren’t there,

How would our relationships fair?

Would society collapse as we know it?

Would dopamine addicts cease to show it?

Maybe we might go outside

To converse and separate the divide

What is stopping that today

We could sync up a different way

With human interactions we were born to do

But synchronisation starts with you

While returning from a sampling operation, a white-out blizzard struck, vanishing the small team around me. Howling over the rasping winds, I huddled together the small team where we quickly realised the blizzard had wisped away all radio communications.

  • I thought it best to stay put and wait for the blizzard to pass. For hours the wind thrashed my tent, which inspired designs for a new wind turbine system. (Supplies -- / Power ++)
  • The compass-led navigator, like the head of a centipede, directed us North toward base. The conditions pulled the ground from beneath them as they fell from a covert cliff, breaking both legs. The shielding cliff face allowed for a signal to reach the rescue team. (Scientists -- / Supplies +)
  • I stayed out alone in the blizzard, the cold chewed through my clothes and body but I gathered valuable data.  (Sanity -- / Scientists +)

I was comissioned to write an episodic story for the Tales app. Here is an extract, it is in a form that demonstrates the coding involved. It is aimed at a younger audience 12-18

In this section, the Player has a limited amount of time stored by the TIME variable. Player can make choices which cost time; "<#act>" indicates a choice and "-> name_example" is the choice and section of code it leads to. The section of code that will be executed after the choice is selected is shown like this ":name_example" followed by a new line.

Character speach starts in caps then a new line e.g.
